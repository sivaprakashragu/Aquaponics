Aquaponics Nutrient Cycle

How fish, bacteria, and plants work together in a closed-loop ecosystem

1 · Fish excrete ammonia (NH₃)
2 · Nitrosomonas convert NH₃ → NO₂⁻
3 · Nitrites (NO₂⁻) pass to Nitrobacter
4 · Nitrobacter convert NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ (plant food)
5 · Plants absorb nitrates, purify water
6 · Clean water recirculates back to fish
🌱

What is Aquaponics?

THE SCIENCE BEHIND THE LIVING LAB

Aquaponics is a closed-loop food production system that combines conventional aquaculture — the raising of fish in tanks — with hydroponics, the cultivation of plants in water. By linking these two systems together, aquaponics creates a self-sustaining ecosystem where every organism plays a vital role in keeping the environment balanced and productive.

The process begins with the fish. As fish feed and grow, they naturally excrete ammonia (NH₃) through their gills and waste. In a traditional fish tank, this ammonia would accumulate and quickly become toxic — but in an aquaponics system, it becomes the engine that drives the entire biological cycle.

The ammonia-rich water flows into a bio-filter, where colonies of naturally occurring bacteria take over. Nitrosomonas bacteria are the first to act — they oxidise ammonia into nitrites (NO₂⁻). While nitrites are still toxic to fish at high concentrations, a second group of bacteria, Nitrobacter, quickly converts them further into nitrates (NO₃⁻), which are far less harmful and, crucially, an excellent fertiliser for plants.

The nitrate-laden water is then pumped up into the grow beds, where plant roots are suspended directly in the nutrient stream. Crops like leafy greens, herbs, tomatoes, and even fruiting vegetables absorb the nitrates as their primary source of nitrogen — the key building block for growth. As the plants absorb these nutrients, they simultaneously filter and purify the water, stripping it of the compounds that would otherwise harm the fish.

This cleaned water is then recirculated back into the fish tank, completing the loop. The entire cycle repeats continuously, 24 hours a day. The result is a system that uses up to 90% less water than conventional agriculture, produces zero chemical runoff, and yields both protein (fish) and vegetables from the same footprint of land and energy.

What makes aquaponics particularly powerful is its biological resilience. The microbial communities that form in a mature system are self-regulating — they expand when ammonia levels rise and stabilise as balance is restored. Maintaining the right temperature, pH, dissolved oxygen, and stocking density are the primary levers that operators manage. This is why real-time sensor monitoring, like the temperature probe connected to this dashboard, is not just useful — it is essential to keeping the entire living system healthy.
